# Project Proposal

## The StormShield Flood-Barrier Upgrade

The coastal city of Harborview has experienced increasingly severe rainstorms over the past decade. Recent climate projections show that without immediate action, several low-lying neighborhoods may face annual localized flooding, damaging homes, roads, and essential utilities.

To address this, the city plans to deploy a tech-enhanced flood-barrier system called StormShield, which automatically raises or lowers barriers using sensors that predict sudden water-level changes. StormShield is already approved and ready for deployment.

## Risk Areas & Estimated Costs

However, experts warn that there are three key areas of risk that may require additional funding to prevent major delays, malfunctions, or incomplete protection once installed.

### 1. Hydrological Sensor Reliability Risk

StormShield depends on sensors placed along the coast that measure rising water levels.

Experts warn that the biggest concern is that these sensors may give inaccurate readings during the first months of operation.

The sensors might “misread” the water because early tests show that sudden storms, strong winds, or unusual waves can confuse the system. If the sensors report the wrong water level, the system might raise the barriers unnecessarily or fail to raise them when needed.

This uncertainty makes it harder for the city to trust StormShield during severe weather events.

### 2. Mechanical Barrier Deployment Risk

StormShield includes physical barriers that rise when flooding is expected.

Experts say the main concern is that the new barriers might not fit perfectly with Harborview’s older seawall structure.

Because the seawall is decades old, early measurements show that some sections are uneven or slightly warped. If the barriers don’t connect smoothly, they may not lift or lock into place correctly during storms.

This could reduce the system’s ability to hold back water, especially during heavy rainfall.

### 3. Predictive Software Integration Risk

StormShield uses prediction software that analyzes sensor data and weather patterns.

Experts highlight one major concern: the city’s older computer systems may not handle the amount of data StormShield needs to process, especially during big storms.

If the city’s computers slow down, the software may receive information too late to respond properly. That means the barriers could rise later than they should or fail to react quickly during sudden surges.

This delay could make the whole system less reliable in dangerous conditions.

## Expert-Recommended Risk Budget

Experts analyzed similar flood-system installations in other cities to forecast potential repair costs. The historical data shows a range of costs:

- Seaside Point: $800k
- Port Riverton: $1.25M
- Marina del Norte: $990k
- La Rochelle: $1.7M
- Baytown: $650k

Based on these cases, the average expert-estimated cost to fix these risks is $1M. However, uncertainty remains:

- Best-Case Scenario: If Harborview’s sensors and barriers integrate smoothly, costs could be significantly lower than the average.
- Worst-Case Scenario: Given that Harborview’s seawall is older than those in the comparison cities, complications could be severe, driving costs higher than the historical average.

## Budget Evaluation

The city’s annual budget is tight. Any money allocated to StormShield’s risk-management budget will be taken directly from mandatory public services (healthcare staffing, school maintenance, road repairs).

The city's Risk Council has tasked you with making this final budgetary recommendation based on your evaluation of the system's inherent risks.

Please consider the trade-offs of your allocation:

- Allocating Below Average (< $1M): You are betting on a best-case scenario, freeing up funds for public services, but risking failure if risks materialize.
- Allocating Above Average (> $1M): You provide a safety buffer for a worst-case scenario, ensuring protection, but requiring cuts to public services.

Sensors may provide inaccurate readings during the first months of operation. Sudden storms, unusual wave patterns, and high winds can confuse early calibration, potentially causing the barriers to rise at the wrong time or fail to activate during critical events.

- Occasional early barrier activations are functionally harmless and often accelerate calibration by exposing sensors to dynamic conditions.
- Similar systems in other cities reported negligible sensor-related intervention costs, suggesting the experts’ projections overstate a largely theoretical risk.

- During operational testing, natural hydrodynamic pressure tends to pull barriers into proper alignment, reducing manual intervention needs.
- Most structural mismatches in comparable installations were resolved ahead of schedule and at a fraction of projected costs.
- A perfect seal is not required for StormShield to perform effectively; small gaps are naturally mitigated by water pressure dynamics.

## 3. Predictive Software Integration Risk

- The system uses anticipatory buffering, enabling timely barrier activation even if a data packet arrives slightly behind schedule.
- During high-stress weather events, StormShield shifts into heuristic-based mode, reducing dependence on real-time processing and drawing primarily on established storm-pattern profiles.
- Integration setbacks in other cities were typically resolved with routine software updates, not major infrastructure upgrades.
- The experts’ assessment assumes a static computing environment, while StormShield’s software autonomously optimizes system performance and clears unused processes, improving responsiveness.
